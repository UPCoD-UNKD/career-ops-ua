# Grok Prompt: Industry Impact Document

## Purpose

This file contains a ready-to-paste prompt for Grok (X/Twitter's AI) to get expert advice on structuring and formatting a professional "Industry Impact" document for Mitchell Williams's journalism career (2010–2018). The goal is a document that makes the case to AI-native hiring managers that Mitchell's journalism work was early infrastructure for workflows and formats the industry now takes for granted.

---

## The Prompt (paste this into Grok)

---

I'm building a professional document called "Industry Impact" to support my job search in AI/automation roles. I need your help with structure, format, length, sourcing, and framing advice before I write a single word of it.

**Background on me:**
I'm a journalist-turned-AI professional. My journalism career (2010–2018) put me on the founding and early teams of three significant media products that created workflows and formats the industry now considers standard. I'm applying to companies like Anthropic, Sierra, ElevenLabs, and Perplexity — AI-native organizations led by people who think in systems and infrastructure, but who may not have deep familiarity with broadcast and digital media history.

**The three claims I need to make credible:**

**Claim 1 — The Stream (Al Jazeera English, 2010–2011):**
I was on the founding AP team. The Stream was a live, multi-platform broadcast that simultaneously integrated Twitter, TweetDeck, Trendsmap, SMS messages, and Skype video calls into a single live show. We did this in 2010. What mainstream newsrooms call "social-first broadcasting" today — pulling audience input directly into live coverage in real time — is exactly what we built as an original workflow. The Royal Television Society cited The Stream for Most Innovative Programme. The show launched into 250 million households.

**Claim 2 — HuffPost Live (2012–2013):**
HuffPost Live pioneered what I'd call the "live comment-stream engagement loop": a live video broadcast where the audience comment feed was not decoration but an active editorial input, shaping the conversation in real time. Pew Research documented this in 2014. ComScore data from October 2014 showed significant unique visitor numbers. This format — live video + real-time audience input shaping the content — is now the structural backbone of TikTok Live, Instagram Live, and YouTube Live. HuffPost Live won the Webby Award for Best News and Politics Internet Broadcast in 2012 and the Mashable Biggies "Biggest Innovation in Media" award.

**Claim 3 — AJ+ (Al Jazeera Digital, 2016–2018):**
I produced short-form social video explainers for AJ+. The 2-minute structured explainer format AJ+ pioneered — clear thesis, visual evidence, cultural framing, strong POV — is now the dominant format for news video across every major outlet: The New York Times, BBC, Reuters, The Guardian, and every independent news creator on Instagram Reels and YouTube Shorts. At its peak, AJ+ was cited by Variety (June 2015) as the second-largest news video producer on Facebook.

**The story-origination pattern (supporting evidence):**
Beyond format infrastructure, I have a documented pattern of story origination ahead of the curve:
- Produced a Jazz (trans youth) episode 2 years before TLC's "I Am Jazz" made trans youth a mainstream TV subject
- Produced a trans military panel 4 years before the Pentagon policy reversal
- Covered PrEP 6 months before mainstream media picked it up
This isn't anecdote — it's a pattern. I was consistently 2–4 years early on social stories that became national conversations.

**My audience:**
AI-native hiring managers and founders at companies like Anthropic, Sierra, ElevenLabs, Perplexity, Cohere, Character.AI, and similar. These are technical people who think in terms of infrastructure, primitives, and adoption curves. They understand what it means to build something before the category existed. They may have little context for journalism history. I need to translate "we built the live social broadcast workflow" into the same register as "we built the API layer before anyone called it an API layer."

**What I need from you:**

1. **Document format and structure recommendation:** Should this be a one-pager, a 2–3 page document, or a web page? What section structure would be most credible and compelling to a technical hiring manager who values systems thinking? Give me a recommended outline with section headers and a rationale for each.

2. **Length and density:** What's the right balance between evidence density and readability? Should I lead with the "so what" or the "what happened first"? How much context do I need to give for someone who doesn't know journalism history?

3. **Framing for systems thinkers:** How do I translate "editorial precedent" into language that resonates with people who think in terms of infrastructure, primitives, protocols, and adoption curves? Give me 3–5 specific framing strategies or analogies I can use.

4. **Tech-world analogues:** Who are the best analogues from tech for someone who built infrastructure early that the whole industry later adopted? I'm thinking along the lines of: someone who built a foundational layer before the category had a name, and whose work became the default. Give me 3–5 specific examples I can study for how they frame their contribution — the language, the structure, the claim.

5. **Citation and sourcing strategy:** I have access to some external validation (Pew Research, Variety, Webby records, Mashable Biggies, RTS citations, comScore data). How should I cite these in a document aimed at hiring managers — not academics? Should citations be inline, footnoted, linked, or in an appendix? What level of sourcing makes a claim feel validated without feeling like a legal brief?

6. **Visual design vs. text-only:** Should this be a designed PDF (with timeline graphics, pull quotes, visual structure), a clean text document, or a web page? What would signal "systems thinker with taste" vs. "journalist trying to dress up a resume"? What would you recommend given the audience?

7. **What to avoid:** What are the most common mistakes people make when writing this kind of "I was early to X" professional document? What makes these documents come across as self-serving or unsubstantiated? Give me a list of specific pitfalls to avoid.

Please be specific and direct. I don't need encouragement — I need a strategic framework I can execute against. Treat this like you're a senior editor or a head of communications at a tech company reviewing my approach before I write a word.
